Two systems, one city

San Francisco has different emergency and public safety systems working in parallel every day. The Police Department records violent crime, such as assaults and robberies, while the Fire Department responds to medical emergencies across the city.

This story asks a simple question: do areas with more violent crime also experience different EMS response times? This matters because crime and medical emergencies are often discussed separately, even though they affect many of the same neighborhoods.

We start from open data published by the City and County of San Francisco: 2.8 million crime incident reports from a merged police dataset and 7.3 million Fire/EMS dispatch records. From these datasets, we focus on Assault and Robbery incidents and Medical Incident unit responses, then compare their spatial and temporal patterns.

The crime dataset covers 2003-2025 and the Fire/EMS dataset covers 2000-2026. In the final analysis and visualizations, we focus on 2016-2025, since this period worked better for comparing the two datasets in a consistent way.

We do not match individual crimes to individual EMS responses. Instead, we compare the two datasets across neighborhoods, time periods, and spatial grid cells.

"Some neighborhoods may face both higher emergency demand and longer response times."

The spatial link: do crime hotspots wait longer?

The two datasets use different geographic labels — the crime data refers to police districts while the Fire/EMS data uses neighborhood names — so a direct join on place names is not possible. Instead, we divided San Francisco into a grid of ~1 km × 1 km cells and assigned both datasets to the same cells using latitude and longitude.

Each point in the scatter plot below represents one grid cell. The horizontal axis shows how many Assault and Robbery incidents occurred there during the analysis period. The vertical axis shows the mean EMS response time for Medical Incident unit responses in the same cell.

Each point is a ~1 km² grid cell · Size = Medical Incident response volume · Color = ALS unit share · Hover to see crime count, EMS response time, response volume, grid ID, and ALS unit share

The scatter plot shows a positive relationship: grid cells with more violent crime tend to have longer mean EMS response times. The Pearson correlation is statistically significant, which suggests that there is a measurable spatial relationship.

This does not mean that crime directly causes slower EMS response. It means that higher crime density and longer response times often appear in the same areas. Other factors, such as call volume, traffic, station location, or neighborhood structure, may also influence response time.

To explore this further, we also use an OLS regression model in the explainer notebook. The model includes crime density, EMS demand, hour of day, and weekend effects. Even with these controls, crime density remains associated with response time.

Yearly trends: 2016-2025

Looking at the annual trends helps us see how violent crime and EMS response time changed during the analysis period. The two patterns do not move perfectly together, but both show important changes around and after 2020.

2020 is an important break in the trend. City activity changed a lot during the COVID-19 period, and both crime patterns and emergency response conditions were affected. In our data, 2020 has fewer violent crime incidents than the years just before it.

From 2021 onwards, mean EMS response time increases and stays higher than it was before 2021. This does not prove that crime changes caused slower response times, but it shows that the post-2020 period is important for understanding the overall pattern.

Key findings

Ten years of data, two emergency systems, one city. Here is what we found.

📍

Crime and Medical Incident activity overlap spatially

The Tenderloin, South of Market, Mission, and Bayview neighborhoods appear as important areas in both the crime and Medical Incident heatmaps. High crime activity and high emergency demand often appear in the same parts of the city.

⏱️

Crime density is linked to response time

At the grid-cell level (~1 km²), areas with more Assault and Robbery incidents often have longer mean EMS response times. The correlation is statistically significant, but it should not be interpreted as causation.

🌙

Time patterns matter

Violent crime is higher on Fridays, Saturdays, and Sundays, and the heatmap shows strong concentrations during late-night weekend hours. EMS response time also changes during the day and week, although it does not peak at exactly the same times as crime.

📉

2020 changes the pattern

The COVID-19 period marks an important break in the yearly trends. Violent crime fell in 2020, while EMS response times increased after 2021 and remained above their earlier levels from 2022 onwards.

🏥

Response time varies by neighborhood

Some outer or less central neighborhoods, such as Lakeshore, Presidio, Visitacion Valley, and Treasure Island, have some of the longest mean response times. Some central high-demand areas, such as Tenderloin and Mission, are faster on average.

📊

The regression supports the spatial pattern

Our OLS regression on 150,000 sampled unit responses suggests that log crime density is associated with EMS response time, even after including EMS demand, hour of day, and weekend effects.

When crime calls, the answer is not the same everywhere

San Francisco's emergency response system is shaped by the geography and daily rhythm of the city. Our analysis shows that violent crime density and EMS response time are related in space: areas with more Assault and Robbery incidents often also show longer average response times.

This does not mean that crime causes slower EMS responses. A more careful interpretation is that crime, emergency demand, geography, and response time are connected in complex ways. Factors such as call volume, traffic, station location, neighborhood layout, and staffing may also play an important role.

Still, the results suggest that public safety and emergency medical planning are useful to study together. Some neighborhoods appear repeatedly in both the crime and Medical Incident maps, and response time also changes by place and time of day. These overlaps are important when thinking about how emergency resources are distributed.

The main takeaway is simple: crime patterns and emergency response patterns overlap in important ways, and looking at them together gives a clearer view of urban safety.
